# Atomic Habits

James Clear's framework for building good habits and breaking bad ones. The core argument: you don't rise to the level of your goals, you fall to the level of your systems.

## The Four Laws of Behavior Change

Every habit, good or bad, follows the same loop: cue → craving → response → reward. To build a habit, make it:

1. **Obvious** (cue) — design your environment so the cue is visible
2. **Attractive** (craving) — pair the habit with something you enjoy
3. **Easy** (response) — reduce friction to the bare minimum
4. **Satisfying** (reward) — give yourself immediate feedback

To break a habit, invert these: make it invisible, unattractive, hard, and unsatisfying.

## Identity-based habits

The most important reframe in the book: instead of setting outcome-based goals ("I want to read 20 books"), focus on identity ("I am a reader"). Every action becomes a vote for the kind of person you want to be.

This matters because identity change is more durable than willpower. You don't skip dessert because you're on a diet — you skip it because you're not that kind of person.

## The Plateau of Latent Potential

Progress feels nonlinear because it is. You make invisible improvements that don't manifest until you cross a threshold — then everything seems to happen at once. Clear calls this the "Valley of Disappointment": you expect linear progress, you get a flat line, you quit right before the breakthrough.

## Habit stacking

Link a new habit to an existing one: "After [current habit], I will [new habit]." Works because existing habits are already strong cues. Works even better when the existing habit and the new one share a context (location, time, emotional state).

## Environment design

Willpower is unreliable. Environment design is more reliable. Put the book on your pillow. Put your guitar in the middle of the room. Hide the chips. The battle is mostly won or lost before the moment of decision.

## 1% better every day

The compounding math: 1.01^365 = 37.78. Marginal gains compound dramatically. The implication is to optimize for systems and consistency, not for heroic individual efforts.
