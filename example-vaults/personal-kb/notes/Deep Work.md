# Deep Work

Cal Newport's argument that the ability to focus without distraction on cognitively demanding tasks is both increasingly rare and increasingly valuable — and that most knowledge workers are systematically destroying their capacity for it.

## The core argument

Shallow work (email, meetings, administrative tasks) crowds out deep work not because it's urgent but because it's easier and more immediately rewarding. Notifications provide constant feedback. Deep work provides feedback on a delay, if at all.

Newport's claim: the people who will thrive in the coming decades are those who can master hard things quickly and produce at an elite level. Both require deep work.

## The four philosophies of deep work

Newport identifies four ways people structure deep work, in order of increasing integration:

1. **Monastic** — eliminate almost all shallow obligations, go deep almost all the time (rare; works for writers, academics with no teaching load)
2. **Bimodal** — divide your time into clearly defined deep and shallow periods (weeks or months at a time deep, rest of the time normal)
3. **Rhythmic** — schedule a fixed daily block for deep work, make it non-negotiable
4. **Journalistic** — drop into deep work whenever you find a gap (requires a trained ability to shift modes quickly; Newport is skeptical this works for most people)

Most people do best starting with the rhythmic approach.

## Attention residue

When you switch from Task A to Task B, part of your attention stays with Task A. This is why multitasking degrades performance even when tasks aren't being done simultaneously. Extended focus on a single task allows the residue to clear.

## The role of boredom

Newport's more provocative claim: you can't switch from shallow to deep work on demand if you spend all your non-working moments seeking stimulation (phone, social media). The capacity for depth is built by tolerating boredom regularly.

## Practical scheduling

- **Depth blocks:** Schedule 90–120 minute blocks, protect them like meetings
- **Shutdown ritual:** A deliberate end to the workday, reviewing open tasks, acknowledging what won't get done today. Trains the mind to actually stop
- **Embrace slow productivity:** Don't optimize for the appearance of busyness

## Tension with always-on cultures

Newport admits his approach is easier for academics and individual contributors than for people in organizations where responsiveness is rewarded. His answer: negotiate explicitly for it, or find the pockets where it's possible. Not satisfying, but honest.
